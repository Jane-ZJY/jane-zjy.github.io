Construction of 3D Cardiac Tissue

Layers, aligned CMs; sychronized beating

Overview

This is my graudate thesis project at Southeast University. I started this work because of my interest in regenerative medicine and my obsession with the unique physiology of heart. Inspired by Yaobin’s paper “Interwoven Aligned Conductive Nanofiber Yarn/Hydrogel Composite Scaffolds for Engineered 3D Cardiac Anisotropy”[1] published in 2017, I was thinking if the designed multi-layered anisotropic structure would make a big difference on the beating function or the maturation of cardiomyocytes. For now, many designs have successfully guided the cellular alignment, but few could individually control the 3D multilayers’ orientation and achieve sufficient cell contact. Herein, I focused on improving the methods for constructing 3D cardiac tissue models which can individually control the orientation of every layer in a 3D environment, and achieve enough contact of the cells inter-/intra-layer.

Results

I designed three models, the one of them was using layers of suspended aligned nanofiber films seeded with cardiomyocytes(CMs) and then encapsulated by hydrogel, to mimic the specific structure and 3D ECM environment of native cardiac tissue (see phased results). This model could induce cells anisotropic arrangement, maturation and directional beating:

i) The 3D cultured neonatal CMs (from SD rats) displayed the orientation and elongation (length: 120μm, aspect ratio: 9:1, on Day 5) in the direction of each film layer, forming a multi-layered tissue with a layer spacing of 60μm.
ii) The 3D cardiac tissue started spontaneously and synchronously contraction within 48 hours. The beating frequency was 1.5~2Hz, and the maximum contraction velocity could reach 7000 μm/s.
iii) Video analysis of the contraction of aligned single-layer and multi-directional three-layer (0°-45°-90°) tissues found that the single-layer tissue contracted in one direction; The three-layer tissue also beat synchronized, but specially, the contract direction has a composition effect by multilayers, resulting in centripetal contraction, which is a similar function with the natural myocardium: shortening simultaneously in all directions.

The other two models will use cell sheet and bio-printing technology. I hope these engineered tissues will be used in myocardial injury treatment, disease models, drug screening, or integrated in organ-on-a-chip in the future.
